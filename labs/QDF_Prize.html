QDF Game Summary

This tutorial outlines the QDF Deterministic Model based on Alipour & Gulliver (2023), detailing how Eve's extra qubit renders quantum states distinguishable to ensure a certain win for Bob in a quantum "Monty Hall" scenario.

It introduces key components including the four measurement variables (fmv) and the 𝒯(i) operator, while highlighting applications in hybrid quantum-classical AI simulators. For more information, please consult the cited SSRN Preprint 4595442.

1. Original Mathematical Definitions: i and j

In Alipour's model, the variables i and j define the state of the system and the interaction between particles:

  • State i (The Box State): A mapping that determines the probability outcome of a particle trap. In the model:
    • i = 0: Ground State (GS), denoting the prize is not present.
    • i = 1: Excited State (ES), denoting the prize is present.
    • |2⟩: Quantum Superposition, representing a state containing both 0 and 1.
  • State j (The Interacting State): Represents the energy state of a particle (where j ≤ i) interacting with Bob. This interaction modifies Bob's state in the metric space 𝒮, influencing his energy levels prior to a phase transition.

2. The Participants & Their Roles

In the Deterministic Picture, participants function as components of a thermodynamic system processing quantum information:

  • Bob (The Atom): The primary participant who must interact with fields to attain a Target State (TS) and win the prize.
  • The Prize (Target Atom): A quantum particle hidden within traps (boxes) that Bob seeks to win.
  • Alice (The Projector): A photonic probe that sets up the game environment, changes prize positions, and projects "qubit" messages.
  • Eve (The Vital Third Party): An invisible observer who intercepts state transfers. She provides an extra qubit that renders hidden information distinguishable, preventing information loss from decoherence.

3. The Deterministic vs. Probabilistic Equation (1c) from Ref. [1]

This equation describes how the introduction of Eve's qubit and the State Transition (ST) operator turns indistinguishable quantum states on the probabilistic side (lefthand) into a clear, predictable outcome for Bob on the deterministic side (righthand) of the equation:

This equation is central to the work of Philip B. Alipour (specifically, Alipour and Gulliver) in their paper Quantum Double-field Model and Application .

The expression is a component of the Deterministic Picture within their Quantum Double-field (QDF) model . Here is the breakdown of the result based on the provided research:

Meaning of the Equation

This result, labeled as Equation (1c) in the paper, describes a process where locally indistinguishable states (those in superposition) are rendered distinguishable by the inclusion of an extra qubit from an external observer, Eve .

  • ( (|0m i⟩ ± |1m' i⟩)Alice/Bob ): Represents the state shared between Alice and Bob, which can be in a ground state (( |0m i ⟩ )) or an excited state (( |1m' i\rangle )) . The "(±)" signifies a Schrödinger’s cat state (superposition) .
  • ( 𝒯(i) ): Defined as the State Transition (ST) operator . Its role is to facilitate the transition from a superposition state to a specific state \( |i_m\rangle \), or vice versa .
  • ( |im iS⟩Eve ): This is the state of Eve's extra qubit, which acts as a probe .

Core Function in Alipour's Work

  • Eliminating Uncertainty (?Y/N): The paper argues that by coupling the system with Eve's state via a tensor product, the model can "eliminate a set of indistinguishable states" in a perfect world (as in observing states at absolute zero temperature or 0K), or ideally, reduce the amount of uncertainty (entropy) to reach near 100% certainity, as a TS at T ≈ 0K. .
  • Role of Eve: Unlike traditional models where Eve is purely a "thief" of information, Alipour's deterministic picture positions Eve as a "vital party" whose state is necessary to reveal hidden global information and prevent information loss from decoherence .
  • Application: This mathematical framework is used to simulate and predict outcomes in Bose-Einstein Condensates (BEC), quantum heat engines, and secure communication protocols .

6. Walkthrough: The QDF Game (Appendix A)

How the formulas apply to a quantum "Monty Hall" scenario:

Phase 1: Setup

Alice hides the prize. In the quantum model, she can change the position (𝔯) using state teleportation. Bob chooses a box (1/3 odds).

Phase 2: The Extra Qubit

Alice opens an empty box. Eve intercepts the transfer and provides Bob with her extra qubit, making the state distinguishable via Equation (1c).

Phase 3: The Switch

Bob use the fmv data and Eve's info to switch boxes. His probability space doubles to 2/3 (or even 8/9 to 1), securing the win.
